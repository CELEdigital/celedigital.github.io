Recently, we have seen a proliferation of laws and legislative proposals worldwide that impose the creation of some form of legal-commercial link or local connection between information and communication technology (ICT) companies—mainly social media platforms—and the countries where they operate. Depending on the case, the chosen method to achieve this objective involves requiring a commercial presence, appointing a local representative, localizing data, or hiring local personnel. These initiatives have not gone unnoticed, and both the private sector and civil society have raised various criticisms, focusing on potential economic impacts as well as the implications for freedom of expression. This trend occurs in a context that, as suggested by Gonzalo Bustos Frati, Pablo Palazzi, and Silvana Rivero, is marked by "profound uncertainty and increasing complexity regarding the future of digital economy governance in general, and intermediary liability in particular." However, this trend primarily intersects with two distinct areas of international law that, while seemingly parallel, may overlap: international economic law and international human rights law. So far, arguments put forward mainly by civil society and academics specializing in digital rights at the international level have tended to label these requirements as hostage clauses—provisions designed to hold representatives accountable by subjecting them to personal jurisdiction, compelling them to execute and enforce local orders of all kinds, including those that may violate human rights. Additionally, civil society and digital rights scholars have warned governments considering such measures about the potential influence their laws could have on other, less democratic or less human rights–respecting legal systems. In this vein, concerns have been raised about how the European Union’s Digital Services Act (DSA) could serve as a model or validation for other governments or legislatures to adopt similar regulations. However, these warnings say little about the legal authority to impose such obligations or their legal advisability from a commercial law perspective. This article aims to analyze current legislative initiatives requiring local representation for ICT companies and their potential consequences, adopting an approach that seeks to bridge two often disconnected branches of international law: international trade law and international human rights law. To this end, the article is structured into three parts. First, we provide a brief overview of the existing initiatives and the main issues surrounding them. The second section examines the legality (or lack thereof) of these requirements from the perspective of international economic law. Finally, the third section addresses criticisms regarding their impact on the human right to freedom of expression. This study concludes that academic research must increasingly recognize the interaction between these legal regimes to develop responses to highly complex issues.
