In the increasingly concentrated online public sphere, the easiest and fastest way to tackle content perceived as dangerous, such as disinformation, is to control its dissemination. In recent years, internet companies have increasingly removed or blocked content—including that of heads of state—labeled expressions, banned the sale of certain goods, demonetized or prohibited content that questions official sources of information, and implemented verification mechanisms, warnings, and contextualization, among other actions. These solutions carry the implicit assumption that false information can be countered with an objective and clear truth. Most solutions targeting social media—both state-imposed, such as the European Union's Digital Services Act (DSA), and self-regulated mechanisms, such as fact-checking—rely on an efficient and functional public sphere. If the public sphere were dysfunctional, the entire system would fail. This article argues that focusing solely on dissemination can encourage censorship and easily lead to abuse. Instead, greater attention should be given to the sources of disinformation. While all disinformation can potentially be harmful, disinformation campaigns are carried out for different purposes, through different means and actors, with distinct obligations and responsibilities, and they are addressed from the perspective of different legal and social science fields.
