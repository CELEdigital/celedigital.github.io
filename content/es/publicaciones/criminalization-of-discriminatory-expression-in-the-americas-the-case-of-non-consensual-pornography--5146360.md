This article, based on a contribution to the 2018 report of the United Nations Special Rapporteur on violence against women, addresses the growing trend in Latin America toward the criminalization of online speech. Often, initiatives that pursue well-intentioned objectives of combating discrimination and violence can, paradoxically, impact the rights of the very groups they seek to protect. In this regard, this paper analyzes, from a freedom of expression perspective, the specific case of legislative initiatives aimed at regulating non-consensual pornography in Argentina—defined as the non-consensual publication of sexual images on the internet or through electronic means. As a conclusion, it is important to highlight that vague and disproportionate provisions in these initiatives conflict with the freedom of expression standards established by the Inter-American Human Rights System. Lastly, this report provides recommendations from a freedom of expression perspective regarding alternative approaches to addressing non-consensual pornography and other forms of violence and discrimination against women. In this line, it recommends exploring: Public-private cooperation : Following the guidelines of the joint declaration on online gender-based violence issued by United Nations representatives, cooperation among various stakeholders is key. As internet intermediaries incorporate different solutions into their policies and terms of service, models such as codes of conduct can be explored as alternatives to legislation. These codes should align with Inter-American standards on freedom of expression and women's rights. Mechanisms that ensure compliance with these codes and transparency in their implementation are crucial for this model to be effective. Training and awareness-raising : A human rights-based approach involves complementary measures that aim to address the structural causes of the problem. These measures include public policies and training initiatives on both gender issues and digital security to combat and prevent violence and discrimination against women. Administrative solutions : In line with the recommendations of the Office of the Special Rapporteur for Freedom of Expression of the Organization of American States, independent entities—similar to watchdog organizations or Argentina’s current Public Defender’s Office for Audiovisual Media—can play a crucial role in processing complaints about potentially discriminatory content and ensuring that any solutions comply with human rights standards. These administrative bodies must not intervene in or monitor content; instead, a collaborative approach between the entity and the author of potentially discriminatory content is recommended to foster awareness of the issue and promote solutions that respect freedom of expression. Punitive responses as a last resort : Criminal law-based solutions should be discouraged due to the risks they pose to the right to freedom of expression. If criminal solutions are implemented, their terms must be clear and specific to avoid the discretionary use of the law or the over-criminalization of legitimate speech that could hinder public debate. Data and statistics : Data on cases of non-consensual pornography and evidence regarding the effectiveness of criminalization-based solutions are scarce in Argentina and many Latin American countries. To understand which aspects need to be addressed, research efforts—by both the private sector and civil society, as well as academia—should focus on generating data about this form of online violence to inform public policy solutions and regulatory measures aimed at addressing the issue.
