The goal of this study is to define the scope of the public forum doctrine in the United States and assess its applicability to social media. Although this research is conducted from a Latin American perspective, the focus on the United States is justified for two reasons. First, one of the earliest explicit references to social media as public forums emerged in the United States. Second, the United States has a well-established body of case law on the public forum doctrine, where its nuances and complexities have been thoroughly analyzed. These perspectives can be valuable for enriching debates on freedom of expression on the internet in Latin America, particularly in understanding why social media platforms are increasingly referred to—both in media and academia—as the new public forums or public squares. First, the study examines the function of this metaphor in law, the characteristics of spaces designated as public forums, and, in particular, how discourse circulates (or should circulate) within them. This section is based on an analysis of U.S. case law, where the concept has been most extensively developed. The next section discusses various cases in which the metaphor of social media as a public forum has been used. The goal of this review is to determine whether its usage is consistent or varies depending on the specific case. In the final section, the study analyzes whether the metaphor accurately represents what it seeks to describe and whether there are potential adjustments or mismatches in its scope. This exercise aims to shed light on the implications of conceptualizing social media as a public forum, particularly regarding the right to freedom of expression and deliberation in a democratic society. Ultimately, the study seeks to determine whether this metaphor provides meaningful contributions to discussions on online speech regulation.
