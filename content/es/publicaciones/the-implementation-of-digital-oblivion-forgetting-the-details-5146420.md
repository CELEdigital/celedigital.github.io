Technological advancement and the expansion of the Internet have transformed the way information is stored, shared and accessed. However, this permanent access poses significant challenges in terms of privacy, reputation and freedom of expression. The “right to be forgotten” emerges as a tool to address these issues, especially when the published information negatively affects a person’s life, even if it is no longer relevant today. The concept of “digital oblivion” gained notoriety with the ruling of the European Court of Justice in 2014 in the Costeja case, which forced Google to deindex certain search results related to irrelevant or harmful personal data. This ruling set a precedent on the role of search engines in managing personal information and the need to balance access to information with individual rights to privacy and good name. Since then, millions of requests for deindexing have been filed in Europe, most of them coming from private citizens. In Latin America, the implementation of the “right to be forgotten” has followed a different path. Unlike in Europe, where Google has developed a private system to process deindexing requests, in the region each case is dealt with individually through judicial or administrative decisions. These decisions have taken diverse approaches: some hold the search engine responsible for deindexing content, while others require the author or the media outlet to remove or update the information. In many cases, the lack of a unified regulatory framework has generated contradictory rulings and has made it difficult to implement an effective “digital oblivion.” This article focuses on the impact of the “right to be forgotten” on freedom of expression, especially in the field of media and the dissemination of information of public interest. It does not address issues related to the deletion of data stored by private companies in databases, but rather the tensions between the deletion of online information and the fundamental rights of access to information. The discussion around this right is in a development phase in Latin America, and there are still questions about its legitimacy, its implementation and its consequences in the digital ecosystem. Judicial decisions in the region have adopted different approaches. In some cases, search engines have been ordered to deindex content, following the European model. In others, the authors of the information have been held directly responsible, demanding the elimination or modification of the content. There are also mixed decisions that combine both approaches. This fragmented panorama has generated uncertainty about the applicability of the "right to be forgotten" and how it should be balanced with other fundamental rights. The document also examines the specific actions involved in "digital oblivion": elimination, updating, obscuring and making invisible information. These actions can involve different levels of responsibility and affect different actors, from search engines to content authors and digital platforms. The lack of consensus in the region on how to implement these actions raises questions about the proportionality of decisions, the impact on freedom of expression, and transparency in decision-making. In this context, it is essential that judges, administrative authorities, and civil society understand the technical implications of the “right to be forgotten” and its impact on the digital environment. Assessing the proportionality of these orders is key to avoiding unnecessary restrictions on freedom of expression. Furthermore, the practices of technology companies also influence the effectiveness of these measures, and there needs to be an open and honest discussion about their implementation. The paper concludes by exploring the concepts of transactive memory and open and closed systems as frameworks for understanding how information is stored and transmitted in the digital age. Transactive memory refers to the externalization of memories to external sources, such as the Internet, while open and closed systems determine the degree of accessibility and modification of information. These concepts are key to assessing the impact of the “right to be forgotten” and its application in different contexts.
