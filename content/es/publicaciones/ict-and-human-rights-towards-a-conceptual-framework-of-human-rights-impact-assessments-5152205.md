The development of Human Rights Impact Assessments (HRIAs) has gained increasing attention as a tool for evaluating the intersection between corporate practices and human rights. Initially framed within the United Nations Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs) , particularly the "Protect, Respect, and Remedy" (PRR) framework developed by John Ruggie, HRIAs have been largely applied in sectors such as extractive industries. However, their potential role in the Information and Communication Technology (ICT) sector remains an emerging and contested domain. This paper examines the evolution, application, and limitations of HRIAs , particularly as they relate to ICT companies, which play a crucial role in shaping modern information ecosystems . The paper begins by tracing the historical development of HRIAs , outlining their foundation in corporate responsibility models and regulatory frameworks at both international and national levels. It highlights two competing approaches to corporate human rights accountability : (1) the push for binding treaties and legal obligations imposed on transnational corporations, and (2) the adoption of voluntary compliance mechanisms , such as the UNGPs and self-regulatory initiatives like the Global Network Initiative (GNI) . The latter approach, which has gained the most traction, emphasizes corporate due diligence and voluntary adherence to human rights norms , a model that has been criticized for its lack of enforcement mechanisms and accountability . The study then explores how HRIAs have been applied within the ICT sector , with a focus on high-profile case studies . For instance, the 2018 Facebook HRIA in Myanmar , conducted in response to concerns about the platform’s role in inciting violence and facilitating hate speech , serves as a key example of how technology companies assess their human rights impact . The findings from such assessments often reveal indirect complicity rather than direct violations , a nuance that raises questions about the effectiveness of corporate-led impact evaluations in mitigating human rights abuses. The paper also considers alternative regulatory models , including co-regulatory and hybrid frameworks , which combine voluntary corporate initiatives with governmental oversight . The European Union’s approach to digital governance , which integrates corporate accountability with regulatory enforcement , is presented as a potential model for ensuring that HRIAs in the ICT sector lead to meaningful changes in corporate practices . The paper concludes that while HRIAs hold promise as a mechanism for assessing corporate human rights impacts , their effectiveness depends on stronger enforcement mechanisms, greater transparency, and meaningful stakeholder participation . The ICT sector, given its global reach and profound influence on fundamental rights such as freedom of expression and privacy , must be at the forefront of efforts to institutionalize human rights impact assessments as a core component of corporate governance .
