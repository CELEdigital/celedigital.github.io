The digital age has transformed access to information and the exercise of fundamental rights, including freedom of expression. However, a significant digital divide persists, exacerbating existing inequalities, particularly for women and marginalized communities in Mexico. This paper explores the intersection between freedom of expression and the digital gender divide in Mexico, analyzing how limited internet access and technological disparities impact the ability of women and non-binary individuals to participate in the digital public sphere. The study begins by outlining the role of the internet as a key enabler of human rights, emphasizing its potential to foster pluralism, enhance public debate, and provide access to knowledge. Despite these advantages, nearly half of the global population remains disconnected, deepening inequalities and exclusion. In Mexico, over 34.2 million people lack internet access, with rural and indigenous communities disproportionately affected. The states of Chiapas, Guerrero, and Puebla exhibit the lowest internet penetration rates, highlighting a stark urban-rural divide. A core focus of this research is the gendered dimension of digital exclusion. Women in Mexico face multiple barriers to internet access, including economic constraints, lack of digital literacy, and sociocultural norms that limit their engagement with technology. Indigenous and rural women are particularly disadvantaged, as structural inequalities restrict their ability to benefit from digital resources. This exclusion not only limits their access to information but also curtails their freedom of expression, reducing their capacity to participate in democratic processes and advocate for their rights. The paper critically examines the Mexican government's past and present initiatives to bridge the digital divide, assessing their effectiveness in promoting equitable internet access. While policies such as Mexico Conectado aimed to expand connectivity, they often failed to address the specific needs of women and marginalized communities. The study argues that reducing the digital gender divide requires a multidimensional approach that goes beyond mere infrastructure expansion. Key considerations include affordability, linguistic inclusivity, local content creation, and accessibility for people with disabilities. To illustrate the real-world impact of digital exclusion, the paper presents a case study on indigenous communities in northern Chiapas. Here, limited connectivity and digital literacy significantly hinder women's access to information, reinforcing social and economic inequalities. The case study highlights the importance of community-led initiatives, such as the "Transparencia Proactiva" project, which seeks to empower indigenous women through digital education and access to public information. The findings underscore the need for targeted policies that incorporate gender perspectives, prioritize digital literacy, and ensure inclusive internet governance. The research concludes by emphasizing that internet access must be framed as a fundamental right, rather than a privilege. Closing the digital gender divide requires concerted efforts from the government, civil society, and the private sector. Policy recommendations include adopting inclusive internet policies, expanding affordable connectivity programs, and integrating gender-sensitive digital literacy initiatives. By addressing these challenges, Mexico can foster a more equitable digital landscape, ensuring that all individuals, regardless of gender or socioeconomic status, can fully exercise their right to freedom of expression in the digital era.
