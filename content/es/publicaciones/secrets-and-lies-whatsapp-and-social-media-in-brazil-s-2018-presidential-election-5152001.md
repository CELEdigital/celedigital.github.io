The 2018 Brazilian presidential elections marked a turning point in the role of digital platforms in political campaigns, with WhatsApp emerging as a central tool for information dissemination, mobilization, and disinformation. This study examines the use of WhatsApp as a mechanism for political communication, focusing on the viralization of false news and its impact on the electoral process. The research contextualizes the political landscape in Brazil leading up to the elections, analyzing the rise of the far-right and the decline of traditional political structures. The study explores how Brazilian institutions and digital platforms managed electoral regulation and addressed disinformation. Unlike previous elections, where television and radio played a dominant role, the 2018 campaign was heavily influenced by social media, with WhatsApp at the forefront. The platform's structural characteristics—such as encrypted messaging, group formation, and mass forwarding capabilities—enabled rapid and unchecked dissemination of political content. This study draws on empirical investigations to assess the extent of WhatsApp’s influence, revealing that a significant proportion of voters, particularly supporters of Jair Bolsonaro, were exposed to and believed in false information spread through the platform. A key focus of this research is the interplay between WhatsApp's closed communication environment and the challenge of tracing the origins and impact of misinformation. Unlike Facebook and other social media platforms where content can be publicly scrutinized, WhatsApp operates within private networks, making it difficult to regulate or counteract disinformation effectively. This opacity facilitated organized campaigns that leveraged automated accounts and mass message distribution, often bypassing electoral regulations. The paper identifies gaps in regulatory approaches, emphasizing the difficulty of addressing misinformation in encrypted spaces while preserving user privacy. It also highlights the legal and institutional responses to these challenges, including efforts by electoral authorities and digital platforms to mitigate the effects of disinformation. Despite interventions such as restricting message forwarding and banning mass message services, the study finds that these measures were insufficient to curb the spread of false narratives, particularly given the strategic use of WhatsApp by political operatives. Finally, the study outlines policy recommendations for safeguarding democratic processes while maintaining the integrity of digital communication platforms. It suggests that future electoral regulations should incorporate mechanisms for increasing transparency in online political campaigns, improving media literacy, and fostering collaboration between governments, technology companies, and civil society. By understanding the dynamics of WhatsApp-driven disinformation, this research contributes to broader discussions on digital electoral integrity and the evolving role of social media in democratic governance.
