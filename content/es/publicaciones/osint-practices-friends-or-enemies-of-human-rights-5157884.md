This research will define OSINT as the collection and analysis of open-source data—whether personal or not—for a specific purpose. That purpose may or may not be for-profit; related to surveillance, scientific or academic research; or purely personal. This paper aims to serve as an initial diagnosis to provide conceptual clarity to the definition of OSINT by analyzing potential violations of fundamental rights, especially those that affect the right to privacy. It will also include international standards that inform both the right to privacy and access to information, which are useful for analyzing the problem. As a preliminary conclusion and the core of this research, the dual nature of the concept is highlighted: OSINT is a problematic practice from the perspective of personal data protection, yet it is also a key tool for strengthening the exercise of freedom of expression in its complementary aspects—both the right to "access" information and the right to "seek" information. Access to open-source information is necessary for research and the production of knowledge, a valuable endeavor across various sectors. To this end, the work will be organized as follows: first, it will shed light on the concept and its different interpretations by separately analyzing the concepts of “open sources” and “intelligence” and their implications. Next, it will address the variety of actors engaged in OSINT, from intelligence agencies, security forces, and other public sector organizations to private companies and individuals. Once the concept and its uses are outlined, the research will examine the various conceptual dilemmas that OSINT presents. Among them, particular emphasis will be placed on the dilemma that, while data from open sources is available to anyone, this does not necessarily mean it can be &quot;processed&quot; from the perspective of personal data protection regulations. Finally, another problem that OSINT raises regarding access to information will be highlighted: although it may lead to violations of the right to privacy and personal data protection, the activities encompassed by OSINT practices are inherently related to the exercise of the right to access to information and, consequently, to the right to seek information. The conclusion will underscore this contradiction, asserting that data collection practices from open sources are a tool that strengthens the exercise of freedom of expression.
