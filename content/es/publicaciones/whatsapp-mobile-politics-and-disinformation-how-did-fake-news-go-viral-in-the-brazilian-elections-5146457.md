This paper examines the role of WhatsApp as a vehicle for the dissemination of disinformation during the 2018 Brazilian presidential elections. Given WhatsApp's widespread adoption in Brazil—where over 120 million users rely on the platform—its use as a political mobilization tool has intensified. The study analyzes how the platform's encrypted, private network structure facilitated the large-scale spread of false information, contributing to the polarization of public opinion and influencing electoral outcomes. The research highlights how WhatsApp's accessibility, combined with economic incentives and networked mobilization strategies, provided political actors with a powerful tool to bypass traditional media channels. The platform's cost-free messaging—enabled by agreements between mobile operators and WhatsApp—allowed for an extensive reach, particularly among lower-income users who may have limited access to other forms of digital communication. This environment created fertile ground for the rapid and widespread circulation of misleading narratives. One of the key case studies presented in this paper is the viral dissemination of false claims surrounding the Brazilian judiciary and electoral process. The paper explores how certain political groups systematically leveraged WhatsApp groups to amplify misleading narratives, creating information echo chambers. The decentralized nature of the platform, coupled with its encrypted communication, made it difficult for fact-checking mechanisms and authorities to counteract disinformation in real-time. The study identifies four central hypotheses regarding the spread of false information on WhatsApp: Bipartite Network Structure : WhatsApp's interconnected group networks facilitate viral dissemination of disinformation. Unlike traditional social media platforms where visibility is algorithmically curated, WhatsApp relies on group dynamics and user engagement to spread content. Network Centrality and Information Flow : The study applies network analysis techniques to identify how disinformation propagates from central groups to peripheral ones. By mapping network structures, the research demonstrates that false information often originates in strategically positioned groups before cascading through broader, less politically engaged communities. Exponential Amplification Through Re-shared Content : The research demonstrates how misinformation spreads exponentially via repeated re-shares, facilitated by intergroup connections. Once key groups initiate a false narrative, the information is rapidly diffused through WhatsApp’s private, encrypted messaging system. Tracing Original Content Sources : The study explores methods for tracking the originators of viral false information by analyzing metadata and hash identifiers associated with media files. This approach offers a potential avenue for legal accountability without compromising user privacy. The findings of this study contribute to the broader debate on digital disinformation and electoral integrity. By examining the interplay between WhatsApp’s technological affordances and political communication strategies, the research underscores the need for new regulatory and policy frameworks to mitigate the impact of disinformation in electoral contexts. It also calls for further interdisciplinary research to develop effective countermeasures against the weaponization of private messaging applications for political manipulation. In conclusion, the paper argues that while WhatsApp provides significant benefits in terms of private communication and accessibility, its use as a tool for political disinformation poses substantial risks to democratic processes. Policymakers, civil society organizations, and technology companies must collaborate to establish strategies that uphold both freedom of expression and electoral transparency while addressing the challenges posed by encrypted digital platforms.
