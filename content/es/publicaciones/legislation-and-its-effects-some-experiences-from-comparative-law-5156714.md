Assessing the impact of enacted laws in terms of human rights is particularly relevant when considering freedom of expression. The role of state regulations in shaping public debate has been a central concern for those who have reflected on freedom of expression from the outset. Regardless of differing views on the state's role in protecting this right, it is undeniable that the state is a key actor. Therefore, it is essential to have mechanisms that allow us to evaluate the consequences of its actions. In this context, understanding how HRIAs function at the parliamentary level becomes especially important. This phenomenon stems from common trends in legislative bodies. For instance, it is not uncommon for legal norms to fail to achieve their intended objectives. Sometimes, they even produce unforeseen consequences. This may result from various factors, such as misunderstandings about the field they seek to regulate, poor drafting, or a lack of knowledge about comparative experiences. These dynamics explain why many legislatures have developed institutional mechanisms to mitigate such errors, improve the quality of legislation, or correct mistakes when they occur. This article addresses the challenges of legislative activity in light of the growing technical complexity of the world we live in. To do so, we first focus on the problematic aspects of the relationship between expert knowledge, representation, and human rights. We then describe the various institutional mechanisms different countries have adopted to address these issues. While this article is not exhaustive and may not cover all existing mechanisms, we believe it provides a solid overview of the changes in the legislative process aimed at reducing errors and increasing the effectiveness of regulations.
