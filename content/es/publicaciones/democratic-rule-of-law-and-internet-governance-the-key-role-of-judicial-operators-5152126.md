A key part of what we understand as multistakeholder internet governance is defined by what we consider to be stakeholders: governments, the private sector, organized civil society, academia, and the technical community. It goes without saying that each of these broad actors conceptually encompasses a wide range of other players with different (and sometimes even opposing) roles and interests. However, it is not uncommon for governance spaces to feature representatives who assume the role of an entire stakeholder group without fully illustrating the inherent diversity within these broader categories. Take, for example, the category of government. Who should occupy the seats designated for this category? Only the Executive Branch? Or should the Legislative and Judicial branches also be included? And within the Executive Branch, which entities should be represented? Foreign ministries? Ministries of ICT? Human rights departments? It quickly becomes clear that the puzzle is more complex than it might initially seem. Nevertheless, recognizing this complexity and developing strategies and policies that aim to incorporate the perspectives of this broad spectrum of stakeholders is essential for strengthening multistakeholder governance. This is particularly crucial when their viewpoints must be considered central to the development of the internet in a way that respects human rights. With this challenge in mind, UNESCO has developed, in partnership with a wide range of international, regional, and national partners, an initiative to deepen dialogue with the judicial systems of Ibero-America around three key topics: freedom of expression, access to public information, and the safety of journalists. Digital rights and internet governance have taken a prominent place within this effort, which initially focused on Latin America but, over the past two years, has expanded into a global program with concrete actions in other continents, particularly Africa. From its inception in 2013, the construction of this strategy has been characterized by a co-creation process. All elements of the initiative, including the knowledge produced, have been developed in close collaboration with the judicial branches of Ibero-America. The content of training courses (both online and in-person), the Training of Trainers Toolkit , and the structure of the Jurisprudence Database —all of which will be revisited later in this text—were created with the active participation of the Ibero-American Judicial Schools Network (RIAEJ) and formally validated by the Ibero-American Judicial Summit (an international cooperation platform for Supreme Court presidents and presidents of Judicial Councils in the region). One of the first points in the decision-making process was determining which topics to prioritize under the broad umbrella of protecting and promoting freedom of expression. The judicial authorities involved had no doubt that internet-related issues should be one of the central themes of discussion.
