In the past four years, online misinformation has become a seemingly novel threat to Western democracies. This threat surfaces in a time of anxiety about the future: democratic systems appear to be going through a crisis as a result of the consolidation of political options that, even if they do not openly reject democratic procedures and formalities, erode them from the inside. It is in this context that the phenomenon of disinformation must be addressed. In general, anxiety about democracy provokes a reaction. In the case of disinformation, the reaction came from the states, researchers, civil society, and the large Internet platforms where misinformation seems to spread. This paper explores the actions of the latter in the face of the claims of the former and frames the question during a key moment about the future of the Internet.
