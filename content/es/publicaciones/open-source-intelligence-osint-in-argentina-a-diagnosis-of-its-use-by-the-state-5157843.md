The current global landscape shows a growing trend in the development, acquisition, and use of mass surveillance technologies by states. The regional context is no different. This trend intensified with the increased use of such technologies following the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. Moreover, states are often reluctant to provide information about their use of surveillance technologies. Cases of illegal state espionage have multiplied across the region, typically targeting political dissidents, human rights defenders, protesters, union members, and journalists. In this context, states are increasingly turning to OSINT for surveillance purposes, marking a notable trend. We believe this practice poses specific dangers regarding human rights. These tools often involve potential violations of fundamental rights and are generally used outside the law. Their use is typically unregulated, and where regulations do exist, they are insufficient. Under broad and vague definitions, intelligence and security authorities monitor "open sources of information" on the internet, such as social media platforms, blogs, magazines, and newspapers. In some cases, the information obtained is organized, systematized, and incorporated into intelligence reports, which may include the creation of citizen profiles. This report aims to provide an initial empirical approach to the state use of OSINT in Argentina. It is part of a broader regional investigation grounded in the premise that gathering information on the use of OSINT practices and techniques across diverse contexts and by various actors is essential. Conducting field research is crucial to determine the extent of these techniques' usage, identify the actors involved, and, where possible, evaluate their impact and effectiveness.
